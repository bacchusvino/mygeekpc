Buy Your Computer Now — Before It Costs More

By Josh Pirtle · March 30, 2026

For 24 years I have been in IT support. I've seen supply crunches, price spikes, and the occasional panic buy. This is different. Every major component inside your next computer is simultaneously getting more expensive, harder to find, or both — and it's happening all at once.

If you need a new computer, a NAS drive, more RAM, or an SSD in 2026, buy it this month. Not this quarter. This month. Here's why.

Every layer of your computer is getting hit

This isn't one shortage. It's all of them, at the same time, feeding off each other.

RAM is in crisis. In August 2025, I paid $108 for a DDR4-3200 kit. That same kit is $499 as of March 2026. That's not inflation. That's a 360% increase in six months. TrendForce revised their Q1 2026 DRAM contract price forecast to a 90-95% quarter-over-quarter increase. Gartner projects combined DRAM and SSD prices will surge 130% this year. The reason? Memory manufacturers have shifted production toward high-bandwidth memory (HBM) for AI accelerators. Every HBM chip consumes significantly more wafer capacity than conventional DDR5. Every wafer that goes to NVIDIA's AI GPUs means less regular RAM for your laptop. HP's interim CEO told investors on their Q1 2026 earnings call that memory costs doubled in a single quarter and now account for 35% of PC build materials. Industry analysts don't expect relief until 2027 or 2028 when new fab capacity comes online.

Hard drives are sold out. Western Digital's CEO confirmed on their Q2 earnings call that the company is effectively sold out of hard drives for all of 2026. They already have long-term agreements locked in with their top customers for 2027 and 2028. Cloud and AI datacenter customers account for 89% of WD's revenue. Consumers? Five percent. Hard drive prices are already up nearly 50% in six months, and it's going to get worse. If you need NAS drives or bulk storage, the clock is ticking.

SSDs are following the same path. NAND prices are surging alongside DRAM because the same manufacturers make both, and they're prioritizing the enterprise AI market. Micron exited the consumer RAM market entirely, pulling the Crucial consumer brand from retail. When one of the three major memory manufacturers decides regular people aren't worth selling to anymore, that tells you everything about where the industry's priorities are.

The chips themselves are getting more expensive. TSMC, the company that manufactures processors for Apple, AMD, Qualcomm, and most of the chips in your devices, is raising prices on its advanced manufacturing nodes for four consecutive years starting in 2026. NVIDIA has overtaken Apple as TSMC's largest customer, and TSMC's capacity expansion is now driven by AI demand, not consumer electronics. Apple, the company with arguably the most buying power in consumer tech, admitted on their earnings call that Q2 performance will be constrained by chip supply. If Apple can't get enough chips, what do you think is happening to other suppliers?

Consumer GPUs are being deprioritized. Both NVIDIA and AMD have delayed next-generation consumer graphics cards. The silicon is simply more profitable in datacenter AI accelerators. NVIDIA's RTX 50-series production was cut 30-40% according to industry reports from Board Channels and Benchlife, because memory manufacturers are prioritizing AI datacenter chips that generate far more revenue per wafer than gaming products.

"That won't affect Dell"

A client told me recently that these shortages were "a me problem." That Dell and HP wouldn't be affected by rising component prices. I understand the thinking, but it's wrong.

Dell doesn't mine silicon. Dell doesn't manufacture RAM. Dell doesn't make hard drives. Dell buys from Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron, AMD, Intel, and Western Digital. Those are the exact same companies raising prices, reallocating production, and exiting consumer markets. There's no secret Dell supply chain. There's no special HP discount. When Samsung raises DRAM prices 90% quarter-over-quarter, that cost lands on Dell's bill of materials and gets passed directly to you.

Apple has actually weathered this better than most PC manufacturers because they secured long-term supply agreements with memory vendors extending through early 2026. Dell, HP, and Lenovo typically work on shorter procurement cycles. That makes them more exposed to spot pricing, which is the most expensive way to buy components in a shortage.

Gartner is already projecting PC shipments will fall up to 10.4% in 2026, driven primarily by skyrocketing RAM pricing. That's not because people don't want computers. It's because the computers are getting too expensive.

The AI industry is devouring the consumer market

Here's what's actually happening. The artificial intelligence buildout is consuming the global supply of memory, storage, and processing silicon. The companies that make these components have done the math: selling to 10 hyperscale AI customers at premium pricing generates more revenue than selling to 100 million consumers at commodity prices. The math isn't close. That's why they're shifting.

Western Digital gets 89% of revenue from Cloud. Micron exited consumer RAM entirely. NVIDIA delayed consumer GPUs to focus on datacenter. Memory manufacturers are converting DDR5 production lines to HBM. TSMC is allocating leading-edge capacity to AI chip customers over consumer chip customers.

Every one of these decisions makes perfect business sense for the company making them. And every one of them makes your next computer more expensive and harder to buy.

What you should do

If your computer is more than 3-4 years old, replace it now. The machine you can buy today at current pricing will cost considerably more by summer. Don't wait for a sale that isn't coming.

If you need more RAM or storage, buy it now. That upgrade you've been putting off? It's not getting cheaper. My $108 RAM kit is now $499. Your window is closing.

If you use a NAS or need bulk storage, stock up on drives now. Western Digital is sold out for the year. Seagate is facing the same demand pressures. Hard drive prices are up 50% and climbing. Buy what you need while you can still find them at current prices.

If you're deciding between Mac and PC, run the real numbers. Don't compare sticker prices. Compare total cost of ownership. Factor in resale value, build quality, power consumption, and how long the machine stays supported. A Mac Mini M4 Pro at $1,999 with 64GB unified memory has no Windows equivalent at that price point. The MacBook Air at $1,099 is priced like a mid-range Windows laptop but built like a premium one. The new MacBook Neo at $599 killed the last "Apple is too expensive" argument.

If you're a business, plan your hardware purchases for 2026 now. Don't wait for Q3 or Q4 budgets. Prices are rising across every component category, and some products are becoming genuinely unavailable. Get purchase orders in while current pricing holds.

What the timeline looks like

March–April 2026: Current pricing still holds in most categories. This is your window.

May–June 2026: First meaningful price increases expected as Q2 procurement contracts reprice at spot rates.

Q3–Q4 2026: Major availability gaps expected as enterprise contracts lock up remaining supply. Some products may simply be unavailable at consumer retail.

The bottom line

This is not me being alarmist. I am being accurate. I'm telling you what I'm seeing across every supplier, every component category, and every price sheet that crosses my desk. The AI industry's demand for silicon, memory, and storage has shifted where these components go and what they cost. Consumer hardware is no longer the priority for the companies that make the parts inside your computer.

This isn't a temporary blip. New manufacturing capacity won't meaningfully ease the shortage until 2027 or 2028. The time to pay the lowest price for a computer this year is today.

Buy now. You'll thank yourself later.
